About me

I am an Astrophysics PhD candidate at UC Irvine. I am planning to graduate this March and currently looking for work in Data Science and Software Development. I have an easy-going and collaborative nature, and a passion for learning. Through my focus on computational solutions to problems on the frontiers of physics, I have acquired experience in software development, machine learning and in a variety of coding languages and analysis tools.

My PhD work focuses on the applications of numerical methods to complex physical systems such as Galaxies and planetary atmospheres. Currently, my research explores the nature of dark matter and the possibility of a new force of nature, a "dark force", that does not interact with the world that we see. I employ N-body simulations to explore the effects of strongly interacting dark matter halos on the scales of dwarf galaxies. I have developed a numerical simulation code to model such interactions and an analysis package for model verification. I am also a contributor to GIZMO, massively-parallel, multi-physics simulation code (http://www.tapir.caltech.edu/~phopkins/Site/GIZMO.html).

Animated evolution of the density and velocity dispersion profiles for an isolated Dark Matter halo assuming self-interactions. The last panel shows the evolution of the core density in normalized units for the simulated halo. The dashed and dotted lines show an analytical prediction for comparison.
A visualisation of a simulated, isolated Dark matter halo assuming self-interactions. Initially, the self-interactions lead to an expansion of the core, causing a drop in mass density, then a collapse of the halo after about 5 billion years. This mechanism could be responsible for the large population of small satellite galaxies orbiting the Milky Way and other massive galaxies.
The range of temperatures on synchronously rotating M-dwarf planets (Ad Leonis and TRAPPIST-1 represent the hottest and coldest M-dwarf stars, respectively). The antistellar (night-side) of M-dwarf planets undergoes complete deglaciation at 2050-2100 W/m^2, while the dayside of the planet ramains temperate at around 25 degree Celsius.
The Eccentric Habitable Zone (EHZ) (the bright yellow region of the plots) shows the range of stellar flux as a function of orbital eccentricity e, that allows for habitable surface temperatures. This EHZ assumes the planet is initially frozen, which greatly shrinks the habitable zone on circular orbits. Orbits with higher eccentricity exhibit larger EHZ.
The Eccentric Habitable Zone (EHZ) (the bright yellow region of the plots) shows the range of stellar flux as a function of orbital eccentricity e, that allows for habitable surface temperatures.
